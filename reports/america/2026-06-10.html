◆ The economy

Data Jun 10, 2026

May Consumer Price Index releases this morning; consensus sees inflation rising to 4.2%

The BLS publishes the May CPI at 8:30am ET today. Consensus estimates have headline inflation rising to roughly 4.2% year-over-year — up from 3.8% in April — with core (excluding food and energy) forecast at about 2.9%. On a monthly basis, analysts expect headline up about 0.5% and core about 0.3%. If the headline prints at 4.2%, it would be the largest year-over-year increase since 2023. Forecasters attribute much of the expected acceleration to higher energy prices linked to the conflict involving Iran.

This briefing was filed before the 8:30am release, so the figures above are forecasts, not the official result — confirm the actual print at the BLS link before relying on it.

Why it matters: Inflation running near 4% touches everyone managing their own money and every small business setting prices. A hotter-than-expected reading typically lifts the dollar, pressures bonds and rate-sensitive stocks, and keeps borrowing costs elevated; a cooler one does the reverse. For anyone weighing a large purchase, a loan, or a financing decision this month, today's number is the data point that moves rates.

Fed Jun 9, 2026

Markets price a near-certain June hold after a firm jobs report

May nonfarm payrolls rose 172,000, beating expectations, with the unemployment rate holding around 4.3%. Steady hiring plus sticky, energy-driven inflation has anchored market-implied odds at roughly 99% for no change in the federal funds rate at the June 16–17 FOMC meeting. The framing among traders: the Fed stays patient while an oil-driven price shock works through, rather than cutting into firm employment data.

Why it matters: A Fed hold means mortgage, auto, and small-business loan rates stay roughly where they are for now — no near-term relief on borrowing costs, but no new squeeze either. The combination of solid jobs and elevated inflation is the classic "no rush to cut" setup that keeps rates higher into the second half of the year.

◆ Charlotte & North Carolina

Local 2026

Charlotte keeps landing corporate headquarters and hubs as NC outpaces the national jobs market

Charlotte's run of business recruitment continues: Japanese bank SMBC Group chose the city for its second U.S. headquarters (~2,000 jobs, a $50.5M investment), SoFi Technologies is setting up a regional hub (225 jobs), and AssetMark Financial is investing $10M for an East Coast hub downtown (252 jobs). North Carolina entered 2026 with a 3.9% unemployment rate — about half a point below the national average — having added more than 80,000 jobs over the prior year; Charlotte's own rate sits near 3.6%. Regional leaders project 2026 as a record growth year, with a pipeline cited at over 7,000 jobs and $1.4B in potential investment.

Why it matters: A tightening, growing Charlotte-metro economy means more established small and mid-sized businesses with budgets — and more competition for talent and customers. Local hiring strength and corporate inflows are the backdrop for the kind of regional demand that lifts service businesses across Gaston and Mecklenburg counties.

◆ Markets & corporate

Deals Jun 9, 2026

Fintech M&A heats up: Payoneer surges on a reported $2.7B takeover interest

Cross-border payments company Payoneer Global surged June 9 on reports of acquisition interest valuing it around $2.7B, reportedly tied to Nuvei. Elsewhere, Snap-on completed its ~$100M cash purchase of Diesel Laptops on June 8. The deals add to a busy 2026 M&A pipeline that includes Netflix's pending ~$83B bid for Warner Bros. Discovery, a ~$55B PIF/Silver Lake offer for Electronic Arts, and Charter's ~$34.5B merger with Cox Communications.

Why it matters: An active M&A market signals confidence and available capital among large firms despite elevated rates — and consolidation in payments and media reshapes the tools and platforms smaller businesses ultimately use. For an individual investor, deal waves move the specific names involved sharply; for everyone else, they're a read on where big capital sees durable value.
