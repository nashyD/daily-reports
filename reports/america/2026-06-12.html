◆ Markets & the economy

IPO Jun 12

SpaceX begins trading on the Nasdaq in the largest IPO ever recorded

SpaceX started trading today under the ticker SPCX, priced at $135 a share for a valuation of roughly $1.77 trillion — larger than Tesla on day one and the biggest IPO in history. The offering is unusual in its retail access: the company set aside about 30% of its public shares for everyday investors, versus the typical 5–10%.

Why it matters: A trillion-dollar listing absorbs a large pool of investor money and sets the tone for a busy IPO window. The oversized retail allocation means far more ordinary investors can buy in directly — and, with a stock this size and this hyped, carry the day-one volatility that comes with it.

Inflation Jun 11

Wholesale prices surged 1.1% in May, pushing annual PPI to 6.5%

The producer price index rose a seasonally adjusted 1.1% in May, well above the 0.7% economists expected, putting wholesale inflation at 6.5% over the past year — the highest since November 2022. Energy drove much of the move. It follows the May consumer price index of 4.2% annual, reported earlier in the week, and signals cost pressure still building in the pipeline.

Why it matters: Producer prices feed into what businesses eventually charge consumers, so a hot PPI hints that the inflation reported at the register may stay sticky. For anyone managing their own money or running a small business, it points to continued pressure on margins and a Fed that has little room to cut.

Markets Jun 11

Stocks rallied as reports of an easing in U.S.–Iran tensions pulled oil fears back

Despite the hot wholesale data, equities jumped June 11 on reports that a U.S.–Iran deal was close — easing the energy-price worries that had been weighing on the market. The Dow rose 1.86% (nearly 930 points) back above 50,000 to 50,848.75, the Nasdaq added 2.54%, the S&P 500 gained 1.75%, and the small-cap Russell 2000 climbed 3.02%.

Why it matters: The bounce shows how much of the recent inflation scare has been tied to energy and Middle East risk rather than the broad economy. If oil settles, some of the inflation pressure eases with it — which is part of why the day's hot PPI didn't sink stocks.

◆ The Fed

Rates next week

The Fed has its full data set and is widely expected to hold

With the May inflation and jobs numbers now in hand, the Federal Reserve heads into next week's policy meeting with markets pricing essentially no chance of a rate change. Sticky inflation and firm employment have, if anything, pushed expectations toward a possible hike later in the year — futures put the odds of an increase at some point in 2026 near 70%, with the October meeting in play.

Why it matters: Rates staying put means borrowing costs — mortgages, business loans, credit lines — hold near current levels for now, with the risk skewed toward higher rather than lower later this year. For households and small businesses, that's a signal to plan financing around "no relief soon."
