◆ Your stack — patch this

Patch 16.2.9 · Jun 9

Next.js is now on 16.2.9 — a string of backported security and stability fixes

The latest stable Next.js is 16.2.9 (June 9), the tail end of a 16.2.x run that backported a security fix (see Vercel's CVE-2026-23869 summary) plus stability work: hydration corrections, router fixes, cache-tag encoding, FormData handling, and a styled-jsx race that could drop styles under concurrent rendering. The 16.2.8/16.2.9 releases were largely administrative — correcting npm dist-tags after a Trusted Publishing hiccup that briefly pointed next@latest at a prerelease.

So what for TSD: Bump every client site to 16.2.9. The cache-tag and FormData fixes matter for the Supabase-backed forms and ISR pages TSD ships, and the dist-tag mess means a recent npm i next@latest could have pulled a prerelease — pin to 16.2.9 explicitly and redeploy. Roll it into a single "managed updates" pass across the portfolio rather than one client at a time.

◆ Security

Supply chain early June

A self-spreading npm worm ("Miasma") compromised dozens of packages via a hijacked maintainer

A new supply-chain attack compromised at least 32 packages published under the @redhat-cloud-services npm namespace — cumulatively ~80,000 weekly downloads. The root cause was a compromised Red Hat employee GitHub account, used to push malicious orphan commits that bypassed code review entirely. The payload, dubbed "Miasma," is derived from the "Mini Shai-Hulud" malware open-sourced in May and carries the same self-replicating, credential-stealing tradecraft that has hit npm repeatedly this year.

So what for TSD: This is the recurring risk of a one-person shop that runs npm install daily. Lock it down: commit lockfiles, use npm ci (not npm install) in CI and on deploy, turn on Dependabot/audit alerts, and don't blindly bump to the newest version of a package the day it drops. A 10-minute hygiene pass now is cheaper than a stolen Vercel or Supabase token later.

◆ The business of tech

Labor Jun 11

Layoffs keep piling up — but the demand is shifting to whoever can build the AI

2026 has logged 247 layoff events affecting ~183,966 workers (roughly 1,136 a day), with Oracle's 30,000 the largest single cut and Amdocs adding 2,900 in mid-June. Against that, ICIMS's June 11 Workforce Report says the headlines mask a surge: demand for roles that build, run, and secure AI systems is accelerating across sectors, not just at AI labs.

So what for TSD: This is the exact tailwind TSD sells into. Every SMB watching the layoff headlines also knows it needs AI capability it can't hire for. A "we are your AI team for $X/month" retainer is the offer that maps to this moment — and it's a clean line for Grant and Bishop to open a cold call with.

Semiconductors 2026 YTD

Chip-startup funding is still sizzling — but the public market is more selective

Investors have poured roughly $10.7B into semiconductor startups so far in 2026 (seed through pre-IPO), on track to top last year, with megarounds dominating — about 51% of disclosed deals above $50M. The public-market read is cooler: Cerebras raised over $5B in its IPO last month and saw shares spike on day one, then drift down about a third from the initial close. The money still believes in the AI-compute buildout; it's getting pickier about price.

So what for TSD: No direct play, but it's the cost backdrop behind every AI feature TSD ships. Compute supply is expanding and competition for Nvidia alternatives is fierce — which over time pushes inference prices down. That's the macro reason the "self-hosted open-weight model" margin play (see today's AI brief) only gets more attractive.
